In seeking to better understand the basics of electromagnetism as described in Griffiths' *Introduction to Electrodynamics*, I had some trouble understanding divergence. I had seen the diagrams‚Äîsources and sinks, very nice. I had heard many times: "divergence measures the rate at which a vector field appears to flow out of a certain region". And I had even thought this meant I had understood divergence.

But the trouble to me was that these made no sense as descriptions of a generic function over a vector space. What does it mean for a function **f: ‚Ñù‚Åø ‚Üí ‚Ñù·µê** to "flow out of a certain region", and why should I believe that this is identical to the sum of its first order partial derivatives with respect to a given basis?

Now this was intriguing, but still unintuitive. On further reading, I found a good explanation of the Jacobian in Tao's *Analysis II* - the generalization of the single-variable derivative to a function **$f: ‚Ñù‚Åø \mapsto ‚Ñù·µê$** begins by asserting that 'f being differentiable at a point x' is equivalent to the assertion that it is 'locally linearly approximable'. That is:

$$
    f(\vec{x}) \approx f(\vec{x_0}) + L(\vec{x} - \vec{x_0})
$$

for some linear map L. We know two things about L - it can be represented by a matrix (true of all linear maps), and it must map $\mathbb{R}^n \mapsto \mathbb{R}^m$ (because $\vec{x} - \vec{x_0}$ is in $\mathbb{R}^n$). Section 6.2 of Tao spells it out, but this $m \times n$ matrix turns out to be exactly the Jacobian ùí•.

We can now tie these together:

- The Jacobian represents the local linear approximation of $f(\vec{x})$.
- For vector fields $F: \mathbb{R}^n \mapsto \mathbb{R}^n$, the divergence is the trace of the Jacobian.
- The trace of a square matrix is the sum of its eigenvalues.

Thus, **divergence measures the infinitesimal rate of volume expansion** - it tells us whether a small region is being stretched (positive divergence) or compressed (negative divergence) by the vector field.
